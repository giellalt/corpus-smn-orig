From autumn 2020, all boys in school grades 5 to 9 will receive a HPV (human papillomavirus) vaccine, which will protect them from many cancers. The vaccine is part of the national vaccination programme and is available free of charge. 

Papillomavirus can spread very easily. Without the HPV vaccine, approximately 80% of people contract a HPV infection at some stage of their life. A small proportion of these infections may become prolonged and lead to cancer, although it is impossible to know who will go on to develop cancer beforehand. Cancers caused by papillomaviruses in men include some head and neck cancers as well as penile and anal cancers. 

The HPV vaccine is effective in fighting the papillomaviruses that are most likely to cause cancer. When boys are vaccinated in addition to girls, more than 90 cases of cancer in men and about 200 in women can be prevented each year.

For the best possible protection against papillomaviruses, those under 15 years of age need two doses of vaccine and those aged 15 and over need three.
